Partnering with COMM 4242: The Design & Governance of Digital Experiments


Is your community or organization interested to collect systematic, causal evidence on an issue that matters to you? Are you interested to learn more about digital experiments, grapple with questions about the power that entails, and support new generations of digital experimenters? If so, consider collaborating with COMM 4242 on research together! This course supports juniors and seniors at Cornell to develop experiments in teams while also thinking and writing about how society can govern and steer the power of behavioral research.

Past collaborators have included the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, WikiLovesAfrica, and online communities including r/feminism,  r/futurology, and r/iama on reddit. We have posted example projects below.

What is a field experiment?

This course focuses on field experiments, research that tests the effect of some hypothesis out in the world. All experiments have at least the following:

  • A clear goal
  • A theory of change that explains the factors that are important to that goal
  • Participants - a group of people, situations, or institutions that are important for that goal
  • An intervention that can be delivered to (or by) the participants to achieve that goal, informed by the theory of change. Interventions can include audit studies.
  • Measures that can be used to detect the outcomes of the interventions with the participants, especially if they are observations of behavior

Projects that aren't an experiment include:

  • Observational research: if the data isn't a randomized trial, it won't work for this class.
  • Surveys: while it's valuable to study people's beliefs and attitudes, they are not the focus of this class
  • Anything where an intervention can't be randomly assigned: if we can't use random assignment, it's not an experiment

What makes a good collaboration partnership?

Since this course supports students to develop research ideas within about 3 months, successful collaborations tend to have the following characteristics:

  • The partner group has a clear goal, and sense of possible interventions. Example: the Cornell Lab of Ornithology knew it wanted to increase participation in citizen science.
  • The partner group already has access to thousands or tens of thousands of participants. Example: The Lab of Ornithology knew they would have a mailing list with nearly ten thousand people.
  • The partner group already has the capacity to measure the outcomes that matter. Example: The Lab of Ornithology already had systems to track how people contribute to Citizen Science, even if they had not previously used that data for experiments.
  • The partner group has resources and staff to support in multiple meetings throughout the semester to support the design and implementation of a study. Example: A staff member at the Lab of Ornithology was able to liaise with the class throughout the semester.
  • The partner group is willing to comply with research ethics standards, including consent and debriefing, where required by Cornell University.
  • The partner group commits to publishing the results and crediting students for their contributions, whatever the results tare.

Not all projects conclude within the semester. Some teams only get as far as designing the study, at which point it's up to the organization to run the study or not. In other cases, the study is started before the semester ends but completed sometime in the summer. In those cases, students have the option to come back to support analysis if they're interested, though it's not required.

What do you get out of collaboration?

  • Students can offer guidance on theories of change, informed by scientific literature
  • Students can define the specific details of the study design, codified into an experiment plan
  • Students can conduct sample size calculations
  • Students can guide decisions about what statistical tests/models to use
  • Students write essays that analyze questions about ethics and social impact related to the project
  • When the study is completed in time, students can support analysis

What do students get out of the collaboration?

  • Applying what their learning to real-wold questions
  • Gaining experience working with a collaborator/client
  • Thinking through a real-world example when

Promoting Inclusion & Participation in an Online Gender Discussion Community

Many users join gender-related discussions online to discuss current events and their personal experiences. However, people sometimes feel unwelcome those communities for two reasons. First of all, they may be interested in participating in constructive discussions, but their opinions differ from the a community's vocal majority. Accordingly, they feel uncomfortable voicing these opinions due to fear of an overwhelmingly negative reaction. Furthermore, as we discovered in a survey, some participants in online gender conversations oppose feminism and wish to make the experience uncomfortable for commenters.

In this ongoing study, two undergraduate students worked with moderators of an online community to test the effects on newcomer participation of interventions that provide first-time participants with more accurate information about the values of the community and its organizers.

Read the report: Reducing the Silencing Role of Harassment in Online Feminism Discussions

In information security, warning messages are used to inform users of potential threats, including malware distribution, phishing, and spam. This team (Ben Kaiser, Kevin Lee, Elena Lucherini, and Frishta Abdul Wali) set out to study if warning messages could influence users to disengage with disinformation websites.

Because the possible message designs too numerous to be tested using randomized trials, this team created a bandit algorithm to sequentially test a large number of possible interventions.

üó≥ Auditing Facebook and Google Election Ad Policies üá∫üá∏

Austin Hounsel developed software to generate advertisements and direct volunteers to test and compare the boundaries of Google and Facebook's election advertising policies. In the class, Austin chose statistical methods and developed an experiment plan in the class. Our findings were published in The Atlantic and will also be submitted in a computer science conference paper (full code, data, and details are available on Github).

In this study, we asked how common these mistakes are and what kinds of ads are mistakenly prohibited by Facebook and Google. Over 23 days, 7 U.S. citizens living inside and outside the United States attempted to publish 477 non-election advertisements that varied in the type of ad, its potentially-mistaken political leaning, and its geographic targeting to a federal or state election voter population. Google did not prohibit any of the ads posted. Facebook prohibited 4.2% of the submitted ads.
